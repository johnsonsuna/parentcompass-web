College admissions is a four-year process, not a senior-year scramble. Families who approach it with a clear year-by-year plan consistently outperform those who start seriously in 11th or 12th grade.

This timeline gives you the full picture — what to focus on each year, what to avoid, and what the key milestones are.

## Grade 9 — Foundation Year

The freshman year matters more than most parents realise. Colleges see all four years of high school, and a weak 9th-grade GPA is difficult to recover from in the cumulative average.

### Academic priorities
- **Build strong study habits now.** The transition from middle school to high school is where many students slip. Academic discipline established in 9th grade pays dividends for four years.
- **Take the most rigorous courses your school offers** that are appropriate for your level. Honour-level courses in core subjects (English, Maths, Science) are typically the right starting point.
- **Protect your GPA.** A 3.8+ cumulative GPA is much easier to maintain than to recover.

### Extracurriculars
Start genuinely — not strategically. Colleges can tell the difference between a student who actually loves robotics and one who joined every club to build a resume. Explore 2–3 activities you're genuinely interested in and go deep over time.

### Tests and milestones
- Take the **PSAT 8/9** if your school offers it — it's good practice and a baseline.
- No SAT prep needed yet.

### Parent focus
Understand your school's course options (AP, honours, IB). Meet the school counsellor and understand how the GPA is calculated (weighted vs. unweighted). Begin thinking about what kinds of colleges you're open to.

## Grade 10 — Direction Year

Sophomore year is when academic direction starts to crystallise and the first serious test milestones arrive.

### Academic priorities
- Increase course rigour. Introduce 1–2 AP courses in subjects where your student is strong.
- Maintain or improve GPA. A 10th-grade GPA slump is recoverable but requires urgency.
- Begin thinking about potential areas of academic interest or intended major — this shapes AP course selection in 11th grade.

### Tests and milestones
- **PSAT 10 (October):** Not high-stakes, but a useful practice and benchmark.
- **SAT/ACT awareness:** No need to take the actual exam, but begin understanding the format. Light practice (1–2 practice tests) in spring of 10th grade is useful.
- **AP exams in May:** If taking an AP course, prepare seriously for the exam. A 4 or 5 signals mastery.

### Extracurriculars
Continue and deepen existing commitments. Look for leadership opportunities within activities you're already in. Summer of 10th grade is a good time for an enriching activity — academic summer programme, research, internship, or meaningful volunteering.

### Parent focus
Begin researching college categories broadly (highly selective, selective, target, safety). Understand early decision vs. early action and the Common App. No decisions needed yet, but awareness matters.

## Grade 11 — Execution Year

Junior year is the most important year of high school for college admissions. The grades and activities visible during 11th grade are what admissions officers see when reading early-decision applications in November of senior year.

### Academic priorities
- **Take your most rigorous schedule.** 3–4 APs in core subjects relevant to your intended major is appropriate for competitive applicants.
- **GPA holds critical weight.** Colleges primarily see cumulative GPA through 11th grade when making admissions decisions.
- Study hard for AP exams — scores of 4 and 5 reinforce the rigour on your transcript.

### Tests and milestones
- **PSAT/NMSQT (October of 11th grade):** This is the National Merit qualifying exam. A high score (Commended or Semifinalist) is a meaningful credential.
- **SAT or ACT:** Most students take their first real SAT or ACT in the spring of 11th grade (March–May). Take one, evaluate scores, and plan retakes if needed.
- **SAT Subject Tests:** Now discontinued. No action needed.
- **AP exams (May):** Priority — 11th-grade AP scores appear on college applications.

### College research
- Begin building a preliminary college list (20–30 schools at this stage, narrowed later).
- Visit campuses in spring of junior year or summer if possible.
- Attend college fairs and information sessions.

### Essays
- Start brainstorming Common App personal statement topics in spring of 11th grade. The summer between junior and senior year is the primary writing window.
- Read good college essays — understand what "voice" and "specificity" mean in this context.

### Recommendations
- **Ask teachers for recommendations in May or June of 11th grade** — before summer. Teachers who know your student well from 10th or 11th grade (typically two, in core academic subjects) are the right choices. Never wait until senior fall to ask.

### Parent focus
Have a frank conversation about college list balance (reach, target, likely). Understand the financial aid landscape — FAFSA and CSS Profile filing timelines. If the family's financial picture is complex, consult a financial aid advisor before senior year.

## Grade 12 — Application Year

Senior year is the execution year. The academic foundations are set. Now the work is translating four years of effort into compelling applications.

### Fall semester (September–January)

**September–October:**
- Finalise college list (typically 10–15 schools: 2–3 reaches, 5–6 targets, 3–4 likelies)
- Complete and submit **Early Decision or Early Action applications** (deadlines typically November 1 or 15)
- Common App opens August 1 — most essays should be drafted by September
- Request transcripts and test scores to be sent to colleges

**November:**
- Early Decision / Early Action deadlines
- Continue drafting Regular Decision supplemental essays
- SAT/ACT retakes if needed (last useful sitting is typically October or November)

**December:**
- Early Decision results (mid-December)
- Continue Regular Decision applications

**January 1–15:**
- Regular Decision deadlines for most schools

**February–March:**
- **FAFSA:** File as early as October 1 of senior year. Many states have early deadlines. Do not wait until spring.
- **CSS Profile:** Required by many private colleges for institutional aid. File early.

### Spring semester (February–May)

- Regular Decision admissions results arrive (late March–early April)
- Compare financial aid offers carefully — net cost matters more than sticker price
- Campus visits to finalist schools (Accepted Students Days)
- **National Decision Day: May 1** — commit to one school, decline all others

### Academics
Maintain strong senior-year grades. Colleges send conditional acceptance letters and do rescind offers for significant senior-year grade drops. "Senioritis" is real — resist it.

## The Year-by-Year Summary

| Year | Key focus | Key milestone |
|---|---|---|
| Grade 9 | GPA foundation, genuine extracurriculars | PSAT 8/9 |
| Grade 10 | Rigour increase, direction-setting | PSAT 10, first AP exams |
| Grade 11 | Peak rigour, testing, research, essay brainstorm | PSAT/NMSQT, SAT/ACT, ask for recs |
| Grade 12 | Applications, essays, FAFSA, decisions | EA/ED by Nov 1–15, RD by Jan 1–15, commit by May 1 |

The families who navigate this most successfully are those who treat each year as its own phase with its own goals — rather than treating the whole thing as something that happens in senior fall.
