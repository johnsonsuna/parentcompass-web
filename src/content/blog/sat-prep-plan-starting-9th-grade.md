Most families start thinking about the SAT in 11th grade. The families who score highest typically start two years earlier — not with intense prep, but with a structured, progressive approach that builds the underlying skills the SAT actually tests.

This guide gives you a grade-by-grade SAT prep roadmap.

## What the SAT Actually Tests

The SAT (now in its digital format since 2024) has two sections:
- **Reading and Writing** — comprehension, vocabulary in context, grammar, evidence analysis
- **Math** — algebra, problem-solving, data analysis, and some advanced maths (geometry, trigonometry)

Total score: 400–1600. Each section scores 200–800.

The SAT is not an IQ test. It tests specific skills that can be learned and practised. Students who improve the most are those who understand what the test rewards and practise those specific patterns consistently.

## The PSAT — Your Preparation Roadmap

The PSAT family of tests serves as a direct preparation pathway for the SAT:

| Test | Taken by | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| PSAT 8/9 | 8th or 9th graders | Baseline assessment |
| PSAT 10 | 10th graders | Practice and benchmark |
| PSAT/NMSQT | 11th graders (October) | National Merit qualifying exam |
| SAT | 11th–12th graders | College admissions |

The PSAT/NMSQT taken in October of 11th grade is the National Merit Scholarship qualifying exam. Students scoring in the top 1% of their state earn a Semifinalist designation — a meaningful academic credential for college applications. The Selection Index (combined section scores) cutoff varies by state, typically ranging from around 207 to 222.

Starting PSAT awareness in 9th grade and using PSAT 10 in 10th grade as a genuine diagnostic gives students two full years to build toward a strong PSAT/NMSQT score in 11th grade.

## Grade 9: Foundation Building (Not Test Prep)

Do not enrol your 9th grader in SAT prep courses. It is too early and the opportunity cost — displacing genuine learning — is not worth it.

What to do in 9th grade:
- **Read widely.** The Reading and Writing section rewards students who read regularly. Non-fiction, news analysis, literary fiction — all of it builds the comprehension and vocabulary skills the SAT tests. 30 minutes of independent reading daily compounds significantly over four years.
- **Master Algebra 1 and move to Algebra 2.** A large portion of SAT Math is algebra. Students who finish Algebra 2 by the end of 9th grade (or 10th grade at the latest) have a significant advantage.
- **Take the PSAT 8/9** if your school offers it. Don't prep for it — just take it as a baseline. Review the results with your student to understand where their natural strengths and gaps are.

## Grade 10: Diagnostic and Direction

10th grade is when intentional preparation begins — not intensive, but structured.

### Spring of 10th grade: First diagnostic SAT
Take one full-length official practice SAT (available free from College Board and Khan Academy) under timed conditions. Score it. This gives you:
- A realistic baseline score
- A breakdown of where points are being lost (content gaps vs. strategy gaps)
- A 12–18 month runway to improve before the actual exam

### Khan Academy SAT prep
College Board's partnership with Khan Academy provides free, personalised SAT prep. It links directly to PSAT results to generate a customised practice plan. Consistent Khan Academy use — 20–30 minutes, 3–4 times per week — over 18 months has been shown to produce meaningful score improvements.

### What to focus on in 10th grade
- **Math:** Complete Algebra 2. Begin Pre-Calculus if possible. The SAT Math does not go beyond Algebra 2 and basic geometry/trigonometry, but fluency matters.
- **Reading and Writing:** Practise active reading. Work on grammar rules that appear on the SAT — sentence structure, punctuation, subject-verb agreement, transitions.

## Grade 11: The Main Event

Junior year is when most students take the SAT for the first time and plan retakes.

### When to take the SAT in 11th grade
- **First sitting:** Spring of 11th grade (March or May) — most common timing
- **Second sitting (if needed):** August before 12th grade, or October/November of 12th grade

Some students take the SAT in fall of 11th grade if they feel prepared, giving more time for retakes.

### PSAT/NMSQT (October of 11th grade)
This is your National Merit opportunity. If your student has been building skills since 9th grade, the PSAT/NMSQT in October should feel like a familiar test at a higher difficulty level — not a cold test.

Prepare specifically for the PSAT/NMSQT from August to October of 11th grade with 2–3 full practice tests and focused skill work.

### How much prep is enough?

Research published by College Board found that students who completed 6–8 hours of Khan Academy practice saw an average score increase of 90+ points compared to a prior PSAT. Students who practised 20+ hours saw average gains of 115+ points. These are averages — individual results vary considerably based on baseline, consistency, and effort quality.

A structured approach:
- **Low-intensity ongoing practice** (9th–10th grade): 1–2 hours/week, focus on content mastery
- **Moderate-intensity prep** (11th grade, fall): 2–3 hours/week, 2–3 practice tests
- **Intensive prep** (6–8 weeks before SAT sitting): 5–8 hours/week, full-length tests under timed conditions, targeted weak-area drills

### SAT vs. ACT

Most students take one or the other (both are accepted at all colleges). The choice comes down to which test format suits your student better:

| SAT | ACT |
|---|---|
| Digital format (as of 2024) | Paper-based (digital option available) |
| More time per question | More questions, faster pace |
| No separate Science section | Has a Science Reasoning section |
| Adaptive (second math module adjusts) | Fixed format |

Take a full-length practice test of each and compare scores to find your natural lean. Most students do not benefit from preparing for both simultaneously.

## Grade 12: Retakes and Finalising

If a student's 11th-grade SAT score is within range of their target, one retake in August or October of 12th grade can improve the score. Most admissions offices see all SAT scores (superscoring — taking the best section scores across sittings — is common at many schools).

If a student's score is far below their target schools' median ranges, reassess the college list rather than banking on a dramatic score improvement in a compressed timeline.

**Note on test-optional policies:** Many colleges remain test-optional following the COVID-era policy changes. Submitting a strong score typically helps — but not submitting a weak score does not hurt at schools that are genuinely test-optional. Research each school's policy individually.

## Building Your Student's SAT Score Target

Work backward from your college list:
1. Identify the median SAT score of admitted students at your target schools (available on each college's Common Data Set)
2. Aim for at or above that median to be competitive
3. Set your prep timeline to reach that target score by spring of 11th grade
